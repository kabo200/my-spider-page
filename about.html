Spider Facts & Knowledge

Click any topic to expand

Spiders are arachnids with eight legs and two body parts: the cephalothorax and the abdomen. Unlike insects which have six legs, spiders belong to the class Arachnida. They help control insect populations by feeding on them and play a crucial role in ecosystem balance worldwide.
Spiders are incredibly adaptable. They live in forests, grasslands, deserts, mountains, wetlands, gardens, and even inside our homes. They have colonised every continent on Earth except Antarctica, making them one of the most widespread predators in the animal kingdom.
Most of the world's 48,000+ spider species are completely harmless to humans. However, a small number — such as the Black Widow, Brown Recluse, and Sydney Funnel Web — possess venom capable of causing serious medical reactions. Deaths from spider bites are very rare when medical treatment is available promptly.
There are over 48,000 known spider species found on every continent except Antarctica. Spider silk is one of the strongest natural materials known — weight for weight, stronger than steel. Some species can live for 20+ years, and the Goliath Birdeater tarantula can have a leg span of up to 30 cm.
Spiders are not insects — they are arachnids. Spiders have up to eight eyes, though many species have poor vision. Jumping spiders, however, have exceptional eyesight. Some spiders can walk on water, and certain aquatic species even build air-bubble "diving bells" to live underwater.
Stay calm when you encounter a spider — they are far more afraid of you. Avoid touching unfamiliar species with bare hands. Shake out shoes, gloves, and clothing left outdoors. Seal cracks and gaps around windows and doors. If bitten by a spider you suspect is venomous, seek medical attention immediately and try to safely photograph it for identification.
